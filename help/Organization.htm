Organizational Structure

Transana is designed to be able to facilitate analysis of very large data collections, and as a result implements an organizational scheme designed to help researchers manage very large corpora. This imposes some minor inconvenience on researchers with small data sets. As a result, it is important to understand how Transana organizes information in the data window. Transana will make more sense once you understand this basic organizational design.

All raw video and audio data is stored under the Series node in Transana's database tree. Transana uses a television metaphor here.

  • A Series is a group of related media files, and is denoted with the  icon. All video files from a particular location, teacher, subject, curriculum, or research project might be gathered in a Series and separated from video files from other locations, teachers, subjects, curricula, or research projects. If you have data from a single small study, you will likely only have one Series, while large research projects can have dozens of different Series which help them manage their data.

  • Within each Series are records for one or more data collection events, which are called Episodes, and are denoted by the  icon. Each time you collect data, you must create a separate Episode definition. If you have multiple media files of the same events, see Working with Multiple Media Files. Otherwise, you probably need to create separate Episodes for separate media files.

  • Each Episode can contain one or more Transcripts, indicated using the  icon. You might choose to store your field notes as one transcript, to have a verbatim transcription as a second transcript, and to have a Jeffersonian-annotated transcription including information about phoneme pronunciation, non-word utterances, pauses, and gestures as a third transcript, each for its own unique analysis. Some researchers use multiple simultaneous transcripts to encapulate information about different analytic layers they wish to explore independently or in conjuction.

From your entire corpus of raw media files, you will identify smaller segments that are interesting to you for particular reasons. These small bits are the analytic segments that encapsulate the analytic meaning you find in your media files. All analytic media segments are stored under the Collections node in Transana's database tree.

  • A Collection is a group of conceptually-related video segments taken from your Episodes. Collections are indicated with the  icon, and are usually named for the common analytic theme you are trying to analyze by looking at the related video clips. (At least, that's how things are in the end. At the beginning of the analytic process, you may well not know what is conceptually significant about your analytic data, so you may start with very vague collection names. However, as your understanding of your data changes over time, one of the ways this emerging understanding is reflected is in changing collection names and shifting collection contents. That is, of course, the nature of qualitative analysis.)

  • Within a Collection are records for one or more analytically-interesting portions of Episodes, which are known as Clips, and which are given the  icon. The bulk of your analytic work in Transana is done at the Clip level through creating, manipulating, and coding clips.

  • Collections can be nested (contained) within other Collections. This allows considerable flexibility in specifying a meaningful analytic structure for your Clips.

As mentioned above, coding Clips is a central analytic activity in Transana. The coding structure you develop is stored under the Keywords node in Transana.

  • A Keyword Group is a group of related Keywords, and is denoted with the  icon. Keyword Group is stored as an integral part of the keyword because a given keyword can have multiple meanings. For example, the keyword Volume means something quite different in the keyword group Sound than it does in the keyword group Geometry. Keyword Groups in Transana are not stored in the database until they have Keywords in them.

  • Keyword Groups contain one or more Keywords, which have the  icon. Keywords can be assigned to Episodes and Clips to describe the analytically-interesting content of the associated video.

  • Keywords may contain Example Clips. These clips should show clear examples of what is meant by the keyword.

One way that you can analyze coded media files is through the exploration of relationships between codes using the Search tool. The results of searches are displayed under the Search node in Transana's database tree.

  • Search Results are the data sets returned when you perform Keyword Searches, and are signified by the  icon. Search Results return Episodes within Series and Clips within Collections that match the search criteria you specify.

Information about the analytic process is stored throughout Transana's database tree in the form of Notes.

  • Notes, as signified by the  icon, are free-form text notes that can be applied to Series, Episodes, Transcripts, Collections, and Clips to allow for a more detailed description of what the item they are attached to means, how it should be used, and where it fits in the analysis. You may create more than one note for each data object. Many researchers use Notes to keep analytic memos within Transana.
